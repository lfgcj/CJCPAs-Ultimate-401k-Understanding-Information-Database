# 401(k) Prohibited Transactions

## Overview

ERISA and the Internal Revenue Code prohibit certain transactions between 401(k) plans and parties in interest. Understanding prohibited transactions is essential for avoiding severe penalties and maintaining plan qualification.

## What are Prohibited Transactions?

### Definition

Prohibited transactions are certain transactions between a plan and a party in interest that are prohibited under ERISA Section 406 and IRC Section 4975.

### Purpose

Prohibited transaction rules protect:
- Plan assets from misuse
- Participants from self-dealing
- Plan from conflicts of interest
- Retirement savings from abuse

### Regulatory Framework

| Authority | Section | Coverage |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| ERISA | Section 406 | Civil violations |
| IRC | Section 4975 | Excise tax |
| DOL | Regulations | Interpretation and exemptions |

## Parties in Interest

### Who is a Party in Interest?

ERISA Section 3(14) defines parties in interest as:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Fiduciaries | Plan administrator, trustee, investment manager |
| Service providers | Recordkeeper, TPA, custodian, auditor |
| Plan sponsor | Employer, company |
| Employees | Employees of plan sponsor |
| Owners | 10%+ shareholders |
| Relatives | Family of above parties |
| Related entities | Entities under common control |
| Unions | Labor organizations with members in plan |

### Disqualified Persons (IRC)

Similar definition under IRC Section 4975:
- Fiduciaries
- Persons providing services
- Employers
- Employee organizations
- 50%+ owners
- Family members
- Controlled entities

### Identifying Parties in Interest

| Relationship | Party in Interest? |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Plan sponsor | Yes |
| Company owners (10%+) | Yes |
| Plan committee members | Yes |
| Recordkeeper | Yes |
| TPA | Yes |
| Custodian | Yes |
| Investment advisor | Yes |
| Company employees | Yes |
| Spouse of committee member | Yes |

## Types of Prohibited Transactions

### ERISA Section 406(a) - Transaction Prohibitions

A fiduciary cannot cause the plan to engage in:

#### 1. Sale, Exchange, or Lease
```
Plan ←→ Party in Interest
Cannot sell, exchange, or lease property
```

#### 2. Loan or Extension of Credit
```
Plan → Party in Interest
Cannot lend money or extend credit
```

#### 3. Furnishing Goods, Services, or Facilities
```
Party in Interest → Plan
Cannot furnish goods/services (except with exemption)
```

#### 4. Transfer of Plan Assets
```
Plan → Party in Interest
Cannot transfer plan assets
```

#### 5. Use of Plan Assets
```
Plan Assets → For benefit of Party in Interest
Cannot use assets to benefit party in interest
```

### Summary Table

| Transaction Type | Prohibition |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Sale/exchange | No sales between plan and party in interest |
| Lease | No leases with parties in interest |
| Loans | No loans to parties in interest |
| Services | No unexempted services |
| Transfers | No asset transfers |
| Use of assets | No improper use |

## Self-Dealing Rules

### ERISA Section 406(b) - Fiduciary Prohibitions

A fiduciary cannot:

#### 1. Deal with Plan Assets for Own Interest
- Cannot use position for personal gain
- Cannot have personal stake in transaction

#### 2. Act on Behalf of Adverse Party
- Cannot represent both sides
- Cannot have conflicting duties

#### 3. Receive Consideration from Transaction Party
- Cannot receive kickbacks
- Cannot receive improper payments

### Examples of Self-Dealing

| Action | Violation |
|--------|-----------|
| Fiduciary receives fee from vendor | Section 406(b) |
| Fiduciary invests plan in own company | Section 406(b) |
| Fiduciary borrows from plan | Section 406(a) and (b) |
| Fiduciary leases property to plan | Section 406(a) and (b) |

## Statutory Exemptions

### ERISA Section 408 Exemptions

Congress provided exemptions for certain transactions:

#### Participant Loans (408(b)(1))
If:
- Available to all participants on reasonable basis
- Not available to HCEs in greater amount
- Made per plan provisions
- Meets IRC Section 72(p) requirements
- Adequately secured
- Reasonable interest rate

#### Reasonable Compensation (408(b)(2))
If:
- Services necessary for plan
- Compensation reasonable
- No duplicate fees
- Services actually rendered

#### Bank Deposits (408(b)(4))
If:
- Fiduciary bank deposits are reasonable
- Bear reasonable interest
- Available for distribution

#### Investment Advice (408(b)(2) and 408(g))
If:
- Meet disclosure requirements
- Comply with PTE 2020-02
- No compensation based on investments

### Summary of Statutory Exemptions

| Exemption | Section | Conditions |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| Participant loans | 408(b)(1) | IRC 72(p), plan terms |
| Services | 408(b)(2) | Reasonable, necessary |
| Loans to ESOP | 408(b)(3) | Specific requirements |
| Bank deposits | 408(b)(4) | Reasonable interest |
| Insurance | 408(b)(5) | Reasonable premiums |
| Ancillary services | 408(b)(6) | Reasonable, necessary |
| Investments | 408(c) | Fair market value |

## Class Exemptions

### DOL Class Exemptions

DOL has issued class exemptions covering:

| Exemption | Coverage |
|-----------|----------|
| PTE 75-1 | Securities transactions |
| PTE 77-3 | Insurance company pooled separate accounts |
| PTE 84-14 | QPAM (Qualified Professional Asset Manager) |
| PTE 90-1 | Bank collective investment funds |
| PTE 91-38 | Insurance company investment accounts |
| PTE 96-23 | INHAM (In-House Asset Manager) |
| PTE 2002-51 | VFCP relief |
| PTE 2020-02 | Investment advice fiduciaries |

### PTE 2020-02 (Investment Advice)

Covers investment advice fiduciaries if:
- Best interest standard met
- Reasonable compensation
- No misleading statements
- Required disclosures made
- Policies and procedures in place

### Individual Exemptions

Plans can apply to DOL for individual exemptions:
- For specific transactions
- Detailed application required
- Public comment period
- DOL approval needed

## Consequences and Penalties

### Excise Tax (IRC 4975)

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Initial tax | 15% of amount involved |
| If not corrected | 100% of amount involved |
| Per year | Tax applies each year uncorrected |

### Amount Involved

The amount involved means:
- For sale/exchange: Greater of amount paid or fair market value
- For loans: Principal amount outstanding
- For services: Excess compensation
- For use of assets: Amount of actual loss (or gain to party)

### Correction

Correction means:
- Undoing the transaction
- Placing plan in position it would have been
- No worse than if transaction never occurred

### ERISA Penalties

| Penalty | Amount |
|---------|--------|
| Fiduciary liability | Personal liability for losses |
| Removal | Can be removed as fiduciary |
| Civil penalties | Up to 20% of recovery |
| Criminal | For willful violations |

### Example Penalty Calculation

```
Late deposit of employee deferrals:
Amount: $50,000
Days late: 30

Prohibited transaction (loan to employer):
Initial tax: $50,000 × 15% = $7,500

If not corrected:
Additional tax: $50,000 × 100% = $50,000

Plus:
- Lost earnings to plan
- VFCP filing if applicable
- Form 5500 reporting
```

## Common Prohibited Transaction Scenarios

### Late Deposit of Contributions

**Description:** Employer fails to timely deposit employee deferrals

**Why Prohibited:** Plan is making an interest-free loan to employer

**Correction:**
- Deposit contributions immediately
- Calculate lost earnings
- Deposit lost earnings
- Report on Schedule H
- Consider VFCP filing

### Improper Use of Forfeitures

**Description:** Forfeitures used contrary to plan document

**Why Prohibited:** Plan assets used improperly

**Correction:**
- Use forfeitures per plan document
- Restore improper amounts
- Amend plan if needed

### Related Party Service Fees

**Description:** Service provider charges excessive fees

**Why Prohibited:** If compensation not reasonable

**Prevention:**
- Benchmark fees
- Document reasonableness
- Review annually

### Self-Directed Brokerage Issues

**Description:** Participant buys prohibited securities

**Possible Violations:**
- Party in interest transactions
- Acquisition of employer securities beyond limits

### Real Estate Transactions

**Description:** Plan purchases property from party in interest

**Why Prohibited:** Sale between plan and party in interest

**Even If:**
- Fair market value paid
- Good deal for plan
- Still prohibited without exemption

## Prevention and Correction

### Prevention Strategies

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|----------------|
| Identify parties | Maintain current list |
| Review transactions | Before completion |
| Train fiduciaries | Annual education |
| Document decisions | Minutes and records |
| Monitor service providers | Regular oversight |
| Fee reviews | Annual benchmarking |

### Detection

| Method | Frequency |
|--------|-----------|
| Contribution timing review | Each payroll |
| Service fee analysis | Annually |
| Related party identification | Annually |
| Transaction review | Ongoing |
| Internal audit | Periodically |
| External audit | Annually |

### Correction Steps

1. **Identify** the transaction
2. **Determine** if exemption applies
3. **Calculate** correction amount
4. **Undo** transaction if possible
5. **Restore** plan position
6. **Report** on Form 5500
7. **Consider** VFCP filing
8. **Prevent** future occurrences

### Voluntary Correction

DOL VFCP covers certain prohibited transactions:
- Late deposits
- Certain loans
- Purchases/sales
- Plan asset use

IRS EPCRS may cover operational failures related to prohibited transactions.

## Reporting Requirements

### Form 5500 Reporting

Schedule H requires reporting:
- Line 4a: Late deposits
- Line 4d: Prohibited transactions
- Schedule G: Nonexempt transactions

### Schedule of Nonexempt Transactions

Must report:
- Identity of party involved
- Relationship to plan
- Description of transaction
- Amount involved
